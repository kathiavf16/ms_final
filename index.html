Introduction


According to the National Geographic Society, “climate change is the long-term alteration of temperature and typical weather patterns in a place. It has also been connected with other damaging weather events such as more frequent and more intense hurricanes, floods, downpours, and winter storms”. These are interesting facts to know, but what causes climate change. The National Geographic Society states that “the cause of current climate change is largely due to human activity, like burning fossil fuels, like natural gas, oil, and coal”

One of the main points I would like to address is the negative impact of climate change on the health of the current and future generations. The World Health Organization (WHO) states, “climate change is impacting human lives and health in a variety of ways."

My research focuses specifically on analyzing air pollution indicators. A 99% of the world population is exposed to bad quality air, and most of us are not even aware of how the quality of air is measured. My goal is to explain the basics of the Air Quality Index and its relationship with climate change to educate an audience that might be interested in learning more about this subject.



About The Data


To conduct this exploratory data analysis I used multiple datasets, but the primary was the World Development Indicators dataset from The World Bank Data Catalog. I used this dataset because The World Bank's mission is committed to reducing poverty and rising to the challenges of climate change. Also, to collect this data, The World Bank works with well recognized international sources, and many important organizations worldwide rely on their research and expertise.


What is the Air Quality Index (AQI)?


Taking into account the focus of this interactive narrative is mostly about the harmful effects of air pollution, it was my goal to only analyze air pollution-related indicators. For this purpose, I used the five major indicators from the Air Quality Index (AQI)

The AQI is the index for reporting air quality, and it is composed mostly of the following five pollutants:

  • Ground-Level Ozone (O3)
  • Particle Matter (PM2.5 and PM10)
  • Carbon Monoxide (CO2)
  • Sulfur Dioxide (SO2)
  • Nitrogen Dioxide (N2O)

    • Learn more about The Air Quality Index - A Guide to Air Quality and Your Health

On the left Y axis are the countries, and on the X axes are the pollutants (CO2, N2O, and, CH4), sorted in descending order starting from the bottom with the highest CO2 emission, which in this case is China followed by the United States, and the India.


    Top 10 countries with the highest tonnes per capita are Qatar, North Korea, and Kuwait




    Total of Emissions vs Tonnes Per Capita


    The heat map is showing the total amount of emissions per country, but I think this is not a fair way to get to the conclusion of who is responsible for generating the most emissions. I believe calculating emissions per capita would give us a clearer picture because air pollution emissions are mostly generated by human action.


    Top 10 top countries with the highest total amount of emissions CO2 are China, the United States, and India.




    Top 10 countries with the highest tonnes per capita are Qatar, North Korea, and Kuwait



    This makes sense because the amount of emissions is relative to the population. The larger the population, the larger the total amount of emissions, but this does not mean this is the most unhealthy environment. For instance, Qatar, North Korea, and Kuwait are really small countries compared to China, the United States, and India. Having such large numbers of tonnes per citizen is concerning.




    Year Change - 2017 vs 2018

    Another aspect of the data I wanted to look at was the variation of amount of emission generated per year. Are these countries making any progress in terms of reducing emissions? For this purpose, I looked at the year change for 2017 vs 2018.


    From the top ten countries, Omar had the largest increase from 0.52% in 2017 to 4.96% in 2018. On the other hand, Saudi Arabia had the largest reduction going from -2.88% in 2017 to -4.83% in 2018.




    Now, looking at the bottom ten countries, Nigeria had the largest increase from 4.15% in 2017 to 15.72% in 2018, and Brazil had the highest decrease going from 1.79% in 2017 to -4.92% in 2018.
