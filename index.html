Introduction


According to the National Geographic Society, “climate change is the long-term alteration of temperature and typical weather patterns in a place. It has also been connected with other damaging weather events such as more frequent and more intense hurricanes, floods, downpours, and winter storms”. These are interesting facts to know, but what causes climate change. The National Geographic Society states that “the cause of current climate change is largely due to human activity, like burning fossil fuels, like natural gas, oil, and coal”

This exploratory data analysis focuses on analyzing air pollution indicators. The World Health Organization (WHO) states a 99% of the world population is exposed to bad quality air, and most of us are not even aware of how the quality of air is measured. My goal is to address the basics concepts of the Air Quality Index and its relationship with climate change to educate an audience that might be interested in learning more about this subject.



About The Data


To conduct this research I used multiple datasets, but the primary was the World Development Indicators dataset from The World Bank Data Catalog. It is important to note The World Bank's mission is committed to reducing poverty and rising to the challenges of climate change. Also, to collect this data, The World Bank works with well recognized international sources, and important organizations worldwide rely on their research and expertise.


What is the Air Quality Index (AQI)?


Taking into account the focus of this research is mostly about the harmful effects of air pollution, it was my goal to only analyze air pollution-related indicators. For this purpose, I used the five major indicators from the Air Quality Index (AQI)

The AQI is the index for reporting air quality, and it is composed mostly of the following five pollutants:

  • Ground-Level Ozone (O3)
  • Particle Matter (PM2.5 and PM10)
  • Carbon Monoxide (CO2)
  • Sulfur Dioxide (SO2)
  • Nitrogen Dioxide (N2O)

    • Learn more about The Air Quality Index - A Guide to Air Quality and Your Health

    Total of Emissions vs Tonnes Per Capita


    The heat map above shows the total amount of emissions per country, but how about calculating the emissions per capita? By calculating the emissions per capita we get an important aspect of the production of emission. As indicated in the climate change definition, most air pollution emissions are generated by human action. Let’s see which countries have been causing the greatest damage per citizen.


    Top 10 top countries with the highest total amount of emissions CO2 (China, United States, and India).




    Top 10 countries with the highest tonnes per capita (Qatar, North Korea, and Kuwait).



    The first chart above shows the total amount of CO2 emissions per country, and the second one show the total of tonnes per citizen. This comparison makes sense because the amount of emissions is relative to the population. The larger the population, the larger the total amount of emissions. For instance, Qatar, North Korea, and Kuwait are really small countries compared to China, the United States, and India. Having such large numbers of tonnes per citizen in these small countries is concerning.
